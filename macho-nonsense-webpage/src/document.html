Sun 5th May 2019

Macho nonsense

What's this?

I wanted to discuss this topic because while I don't encounter it frequently, I think the effect it has on other people and psychological safety is really unacceptable. I am not ashamed to admit that I am very afflicted by this nonsense sadly - of course you might be fortunate enough to think "Oh I didn't think Rob was like that", but that would probably be because I'm always getting better! I will inevitably miss some things, but that's part and parcel with writing your thoughts down, so I won't feel bad.

What effect does macho nonsense have on men?

Many men have learned that there are some properties that must be true about them if they are to be accepted as valuable people. In bad cases, they learn that these properties form the core of their worth as a person. The catch is that these things are not true in any consistent interpretation of reality. This requirement of cognitive dissonance causes men to behave in really strange ways! When their reality (the one they construct to protect their ego) is threatened they become defensive. This fear of being exposed as a normal human and not an action-man like James Bond is, as far as I can think of, the cause of the variety of interesting but unfortunate behaviours:

  • Defensive upon indications of having been incorrect about something at some point
  • A desire to express that they are really strong and smart
  • Pride in unhealthy and unnecessary exertion

And what effect does that have on other people and psychological safety? : Defensiveness

Not a good one! When people feel that they are on eggshells trying to navigate the male ego they are unlikely to produce the kind of fast, quality feedback that a team needs in order to thrive! If problems occur and are not addressed because the solution is to tell Robinho that he was wrong and needs to just stop, but we think that'll cause him to sulk and be worse than useless, then maybe we'll just leave it. That's a bad outcome, and it happened because Robinho's macho nonsense caused the team to feel unsafe.

And what effect does that have on other people and psychological safety? : Peacocking

Insecure men dominating conversations is a phenomenon that has the potential to cause other team mates to feel unheard and feel that maybe what they have to say isn't important. This is obviously very bad for safety. Additionally, if the man in question is of seniority and is for whatever reason looked up to, the quality of excessively occupying space in a discussion will be transferred to the team as something that senior people are meant to do, which extends this problem. The best people I know speak less and listen more, and when they do speak, you don't get the feeling that it's about them.

And what effect does that have on other people and psychological safety? : Unsustainable pace

I have been and continue to be proud of sleeping in the library and washing at the gym during my final exam week of university. This is foolish behaviour. A pace like this is never sustainable and will damage performance over the kinds of time-spans required to deliver valuable software. Additionally, such behaviour may put pressure on team mates to demonstrate similar levels of presenteeism, causing further burnout and damage to the team's performance, not to mention their mental health! This kind of macho nonsense should undoubtedly be discouraged.

Summary

Macho nonsense describes a set of nonsense beliefs held by men which cause them to behave oddly, in ways that themselves may be described as macho nonsense. These behaviours undermine psychological safety and in turn the mental health and productivity of the team. Macho nonsense should be discouraged and men should be coached and mentored to help them not behave in this way. Personally I favour a more extreme position: Stop hiring men, or at least screen them in the interview. I believe Thoughtworks already incorporates a combination of these strategies - a 50:50 gender balanced engineering workforce and a "personality interview". I took that interview about a year and a half ago and failed it - I hope I would pass now.
